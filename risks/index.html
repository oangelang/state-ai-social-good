The real risks

A site about AI for social good that doesn't name the harms is a brochure, not a field resource. These six categories are documented, active, and inadequately addressed. Updated when significant new evidence emerges.

01
Bias & Discrimination
AI systems trained on historical data inherit and amplify existing inequalities. Hiring tools, credit scoring, medical diagnostics, and criminal justice have all produced documented discriminatory outcomes — even after explicit debiasing attempts. The core problem: if the training data reflects past inequity, the model learns to reproduce it. Debiasing a model without addressing the data source is cosmetic.
55% of AI experts and 55% of the US public are highly worried about AI bias — the rare issue they agree on. Pew Research, 2025
Documented cases: Amazon's recruiting tool penalized women's resumes (2018, scrapped); ProPublica's COMPAS analysis showed Black defendants scored higher-risk at twice the rate of white defendants; multiple hiring AI audits in 2024–25 found racial disparities persisted post-remediation.
02
Epistemic Risk
Deepfakes, synthetic media, and AI-generated misinformation erode the shared factual basis of public life. The problem isn't just false content — it's the collapse of confidence in authentic content. When everything can be faked, nothing can be trusted. This destabilizes democratic institutions, public health communication, and crisis response more broadly than any single piece of misinformation.
70% of AI experts are highly worried about AI-generated inaccurate information — the top shared concern across groups. Pew Research, 2025
Scale: AI impersonation scams cost $5.3B in 2025. A deepfake of a finance minister was used to defraud a retired doctor. Synthetic audio of politicians has been used in election interference in multiple countries.
03
Environmental Cost
AI's resource footprint is growing faster than most climate commitments. Training large models requires enormous compute, which requires enormous energy and water. The companies building AI for climate solutions are often themselves significant contributors to the problem. This creates a genuine tension that the field mostly avoids discussing honestly.
AI carbon footprint: 32.6–79.7M tons CO₂ in 2025. Water: up to 764 billion liters. US data centers projected at 12% of national electricity by 2028. ScienceDirect, 2025
Specifics: Training GPT-3 evaporated ~700,000 liters of freshwater. A single ChatGPT conversation uses ~10x the water of a standard Google search. Microsoft's 2024 sustainability report showed a 30% increase in water consumption year-over-year.
04
Economic Disruption
Job displacement is uneven and accelerating. The communities least able to absorb economic disruption bear the most risk. Productivity gains from AI are concentrating at the top of the income distribution. Entry-level and administrative roles — the roles that build career foundations — are declining fastest. The social impact sector is not immune: grant writing, program reporting, and impact measurement work are all being automated.
56% of US adults are very concerned about AI eliminating jobs. Only 25% of AI experts share that concern — a gap that reflects who benefits from automation. Pew Research, 2025
Sector-specific: McKinsey estimates 60–70% of current work activities are technically automatable. Customer service, data entry, and content moderation — disproportionately held by workers of color and Global South workers — are the most exposed.
05
Security & Warfare
AI is lowering the barrier for cyberattacks, biological and chemical weapon design, and autonomous weapons development. Facial recognition deployed in conflict zones has caused wrongful detentions. Biometric data collected during humanitarian crises has been accessed by state actors. The humanitarian sector has a specific vulnerability: it collects sensitive data on the world's most vulnerable people and operates in contexts where that data can be weaponized.
AI deepfake-as-a-service financial fraud caused billions in losses in 2025. Documented state misuse of humanitarian biometric data in multiple conflicts. AIhub, 2026
Humanitarian data risk: UNHCR iris scan data has been documented as accessed by border agencies. The field has no consistent standard for data minimization, retention, or destruction in crisis contexts.
06
Governance & Power
AI capability is concentrating in a handful of companies and a few governments. The communities most affected by AI decisions have the least power to shape them. Regulation is fragmented and slow. The EU AI Act is the most comprehensive framework — enforcement begins August 2026. The US still has no federal AI law. 83% of AI investment in Africa concentrates in just four countries. The Global South is building the infrastructure of AI deployment without building the governance capacity to oversee it.
45 US states proposed AI bills in 2024. 31 enacted laws. Zero at the federal level. Cimplifi, 2025
What's missing: Independent auditing infrastructure. Mandatory incident reporting. Community representation in model governance. Any mechanism for affected communities to contest AI decisions at scale.

What we're watching

These risks aren't static. The monthly deep audit workflow tracks new incidents, regulatory changes, and research findings that update our understanding of each category. The risks section is updated when evidence materially changes — not on a fixed schedule.
